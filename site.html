A heritage assemblage shaped by river, rail, and a century of regional industry.

The site comprises four contiguous parcels in the historic Tumwater district, bound by the Deschutes River, the Union Pacific rail corridor, Custer Way SW, and the Schmidt House parcel held by the Olympia Tumwater Foundation. The brew tower outparcel within Parcel B is owned by the City of Tumwater and is excluded from this transaction.

Heritage & Setting

A century of industrial and cultural history.

1906

The Olympia Brewhouse

The brewhouse complex on Parcel B was constructed between 1905 and 1906 and operated as the Olympia Brewing Company facility for the better part of the 20th century. The brick-and-tile main structure, with its distinctive tower, is one of the most photographed industrial buildings in the South Sound and a designated heritage landmark in Tumwater’s historic district.

River

The Deschutes & Tumwater Falls

The site is bounded to the north and west by the Deschutes River, with direct frontage on the lower falls. The Deschutes is a Type F fish-bearing stream subject to federal Endangered Species Act review and treaty fishing rights. The river is the site’s defining natural amenity and its defining regulatory constraint.

Adj.

Schmidt House & Historical Park

The Schmidt House (Olympia Tumwater Foundation) sits directly south of Parcel B and is preserved in place. Tumwater Historical Park lies across the river to the north. These adjacencies establish a heritage and open-space context that supports destination programming and limits incompatible neighboring uses.

Access

Custer Way & the Boston Street Descent

Primary access is from Custer Way SW — an arterial collector — via Boston Street SE, which descends approximately 110 vertical feet to the site over varying grades. KPFF’s civil due diligence identifies the road as the principal infrastructure investment required at acquisition.

Cultural & Ecological Context

The Deschutes Estuary restoration.

The Washington Department of Enterprise Services has identified the Estuary Alternative as the preferred long-term management option for the Capitol Lake basin. The plan removes the 5th Avenue Dam — constructed in 1951 — and restores approximately 260 acres of tidal estuary where the Deschutes River meets Puget Sound. The South Basin of the planned estuary sits immediately downstream of the brewery site.

Today · Capitol Lake

Freshwater impoundment, dam-controlled

The current Capitol Lake is a 260-acre freshwater impoundment behind the 5th Avenue Dam, with degraded water quality, invasive species pressure, and ongoing sediment management costs. Riparian wetlands flank the upper Deschutes corridor including the site’s northern boundary.

Post-Restoration · Deschutes Estuary

Tidal salt marsh, salmon habitat restored

Following dam removal, tidal flow returns to the basin. Freshwater wetlands convert to estuarine wetlands. Salmon and steelhead access expands. The site becomes the gateway parcel to the largest estuary restoration in the South Puget Sound — with significant cultural, treaty fishing, and conservation alignment for tribal ownership.

Figure 02 The planned 260-acre Deschutes Estuary restoration. The project site (upper right) sits at the head of the South Basin, becoming the gateway parcel to the largest estuary restoration in South Puget Sound.Source: WA Department of Enterprise Services, Capitol Lake – Deschutes Estuary Final EIS, October 2022
